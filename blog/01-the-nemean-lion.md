# The Nemean Lion

The oracle at Delphi had been clear. Twelve labors, served under Eurystheus of Mycenae, and only then would the blood-debt be paid. Hercules had killed his own children in a madness sent by Hera. No prayer, no offering had touched the weight of it. The Pythia delivered the sentence without ornament.

Eurystheus was a small man who had inherited a kingdom that should by birth-order have been Hercules'. He had spent his youth being told that the strongest man alive was somewhere out there, working off a crime, and would one day walk through his palace gates. When Hercules did walk through them — kneeling in the dust of the great hall — Eurystheus could barely speak. He chose the first labor in a hurry and chose it badly.

"The Nemean Lion," he said. "Bring me its hide."

The court went quiet. The lion of Nemea had been born of Typhon and Echidna, the same dark line that had bred the Hydra. Its golden hide turned aside every arrow ever shot at it. Whole villages around the valley had moved away. Hercules bowed, said yes, and left the same afternoon.

He arrived in late summer and set up camp on a hill above the lion's hunting ground. For days he watched. The lion came down each evening to drink, picked off whatever stray animal it found, and went back into the rocks before dawn. Hercules studied its routes. He noted where the wind broke around the limestone. He saw the cave with two mouths.

On the first day of the hunt he shot the lion through the chest. The arrow shattered against the pelt. The lion turned, looked at him for the time it takes a man to draw breath, and walked away without hurrying.

On the second day Hercules tried his sword. The blade folded the way soft iron will fold against stone. The lion swatted him backwards and walked off again, almost bored.

For twenty more days, Hercules tried smarter and smarter approaches. He drove the lion with fire. He set pitfalls in the goat-paths. He dropped boulders from cliffs. Nothing held. By the end of the month he understood that there was no weapon in the world that would do this job. He would have to do it with his hands.

He chose his evening. He drove the lion back to its cave at the last hour of the day. The cave had two mouths. Hercules sealed the eastern one with boulders the size of altars. Then he stripped off his pack, his bow, and his sword, and walked into the western mouth alone.

The fight inside the cave is something nobody saw and nobody could tell properly afterward. Hercules came out at sunrise, bleeding from a dozen places, with the lion's body across his shoulders. He had broken its neck with his hands.

He sat with the body in the sun for an hour, working out the problem of the hide. No blade he had would cut it. He tried for some time before he understood: the only thing in the world that could cut the lion's skin was the lion. He used one of its own claws, hooked under his thumb, to open the body up. The skin came off in one piece. He dried it in the sun for two days, then slung it over his shoulders.

The pelt fit him as if it had been waiting for him. The empty jaws came up over his head like a hood; the forepaws crossed at his chest. From that day forward, Hercules walked the world in a coat the gods themselves could not cut.

When he returned to Mycenae, Eurystheus screamed and climbed inside the great bronze jar that stood at the side of the throne. From inside the jar, muffled and shaking, the king sent word through his herald: the labor was accepted. The next was already chosen.

*Don't bring it through the door. Leave it at the gate.*
