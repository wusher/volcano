# The Lernaean Hydra

After the Nemean Lion came a worse one. The Lernaean Hydra lived in the salt marshes south of Argos, in a swamp the locals had given up on years ago. Cattle that wandered too close did not come back. Travelers were waylaid by what the priests called a poison-breath. The mouths of the spring of Amymone, sacred to Poseidon, had filled with mud and stink.

Eurystheus delivered the second labor through his herald, from inside or near the bronze jar — Hercules was no longer sure which. "Kill the Hydra of Lerna," the herald read, "and bring proof."

Hercules took his nephew Iolaus along for this one. Iolaus was sixteen, devoted, useful with horses and fast on his feet. The two of them drove a chariot down to Lerna in two days. The smell of the swamp met them a mile out, salt and rot and something underneath that Hercules could not name.

They left the chariot at the edge of the reeds. Hercules walked in alone first, to see what he was facing. He found the Hydra coiled around the spring like a knot of cables. Nine heads. The middle one — larger than the others, ringed at the neck with hard scales — was, the locals had warned, immortal.

He came back to the chariot and told Iolaus to make fire. Then he picked up his sword and went in.

The first head he severed dropped into the water and floated. Two new heads grew from the stump while he was still watching. They grew fully formed and biting. Hercules cut them both off. Four grew back from those stumps. He stood for a long moment in waist-deep water with a tangle of new snakes hissing at him and understood that he was losing.

He shouted for Iolaus.

The boy came running with a burning torch in each hand. "When I cut," Hercules said, water up to his thighs, "you burn. Don't think. Burn."

The boy did. After every cut, Iolaus pressed a brand to the stump. The flesh, cauterized, would not bud. The heads went one by one — five, six, seven — and the body of the Hydra slowly stopped writhing. Hercules' sword arm shook. Iolaus' face was black with smoke. Neither stopped.

The eighth head went. Then they were standing over the immortal one. It would not die. Hercules severed it anyway and they sealed the wound with everything they had — fire, then mud, then a flat stone. The hero carried that still-living, still-hissing head up the road to a place between Lerna and Elaeus and buried it under a boulder. It is there now, they say, in the deep dirt, still alive.

Before they left, Hercules did one more thing. He dipped a quiver of arrows in the Hydra's blood. The blood was thick, almost black, and stank of something he had no word for. He sealed the arrows in oiled cloth and packed them carefully. He had no way of knowing that the same venom would, decades later, find its way back to him through a centaur's wound and a robe given by a wife who thought she was saving her marriage. The Hydra was killing him in slow motion from this day on.

They returned to Mycenae. Iolaus stood proudly behind Hercules in the great hall. Eurystheus, from inside the bronze jar — he had taken to staying near it now — heard the report and rejected the labor.

"The boy helped," he said. "It was supposed to be done alone. This labor doesn't count."

Hercules did not argue. The boy went home to his mother in the morning. The hero set down his pack, opened it, took out two of the new poisoned arrows, and put them in his quiver. He waited for the next labor with a steadiness that had not been there before.

The thing he had felt in the swamp — the moment when he had been alone with the impossible and had remembered, just in time, that he had help — was something he would not forget. It was a different kind of victory than killing a lion in a cave with his hands. He liked it less. He needed it more.
