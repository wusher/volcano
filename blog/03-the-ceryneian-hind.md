# The Ceryneian Hind

The third labor was a problem of patience.

The Ceryneian Hind was sacred to Artemis. She was one of five such creatures the goddess had hunted as a girl and tamed — golden-horned, bronze-hoofed, faster than any beast on land. Four had been yoked to Artemis' chariot. The fifth had refused. That fifth still ran in the hills of Arcadia, untouched, untouchable.

Eurystheus sent Hercules to bring her back alive. "Don't kill her," he said. "Bring her unharmed. Or it doesn't count."

It was a clever choice for once. Killing the hind would offend Artemis, and an offended Artemis killed people in interesting ways. Failing to capture her would offend Eurystheus, which by now meant losing the year's work. The narrow path between the two was the labor.

Hercules left Mycenae in early autumn. He found the hind's tracks within a week — bronze hoofprints, small and deep, on a cold morning along the Ladon river. He followed them.

She let him close. That was the worst of it. Every dawn he would find her standing on a ridge or by a stream, her head turned just enough to acknowledge him, and just as he raised his bow she would explode into motion. By the time the arrow was nocked she was a brown blur on the next hill. The third time it happened he understood that she was *playing*. The fifth time, he understood that she was teaching him.

He chased her north. Through the snow-fields of Argos, through olive groves in the rain, across rivers that froze and thawed with the season. Winter came. Hercules slept in caves. He killed deer for food and rationed grain when he could find it. He grew his beard out and stopped speaking aloud unless he had to.

He chased her south. Across Arcadia again, down to the coast, back inland. She showed no sign of tiring. The hero's boots wore through twice. He patched them with leather from his pack and kept going. Once a shepherd, seeing him pass on a high path, called out an offer of food. Hercules waved without turning his head. He could not afford to lose the line.

A full year passed.

In the second autumn — exactly twelve months from the start — Hercules came on her at a small stream in the foothills of Mount Artemision. She was drinking. The wind was in his face. He could see, for the first time in a year, how to make the shot work.

He drew very slowly. He aimed at her front leg, the gap between sinew and bone where an arrow would pin without breaking. He breathed out and released.

The arrow caught her exactly where he wanted. She fell. She did not bleed much. He ran to her, lifted her across his shoulders, and felt her tremble against his neck as he started the long walk back.

Artemis met him on the road.

She came out of the trees on his left as if she had always been there, bow across her back, expression unreadable. Apollo, her brother, walked beside her. Both of them watched Hercules without speaking.

The hero went to his knees. The hind was still across his shoulders, head against his ear, breathing.

"I am at the command of Eurystheus of Mycenae," he said. "I am at the will of the gods. I bring her unhurt. The arrow passed where it could heal. I will free her at the gates of the city, the moment the king sees her. I swear by you."

Artemis looked at him for some time. Then she walked over, ran her hand down the hind's flank, and stepped back.

"Pass," she said. "Do not return."

He did not return. He carried the hind south. At the gates of Mycenae he stood before Eurystheus, who came out — for once — to see the labor in person. The king reached for the hind's golden antler. Hercules slipped his fingers, gave the hind a single push, and she bolted. She was gone before the king had closed his hand.

Eurystheus shouted. He had not, the king said, technically *received* the animal.

Hercules pointed out, calmly, that the terms had been delivery alive and unharmed. The terms had been met. The king could chase her himself if he wanted.

Eurystheus considered this. He decided not to chase her. The labor counted.
