# The Erymanthian Boar

The fourth labor was supposed to be a simple capture: bring back the enormous boar that was tearing up the slopes of Mount Erymanthos in winter. Hercules left Mycenae in the first week of January. Snow on the high passes. The boar had been killing shepherds for three years and the king of the region had given up.

He climbed for two days. On the second night, freezing and tired, he stopped at the cave of Pholus, a centaur known to be hospitable to travelers. Pholus came out to meet him in the firelight. They had not seen each other before, but the centaur knew his face — everyone knew his face by now — and led him inside.

Pholus set out cold meat for the hero and uncooked meat for himself. They ate in companionable silence for a while. Then Hercules asked, in the polite way of a guest, whether there might be wine.

Pholus hesitated. There was, he said, a jar of wine in the back of the cave. It belonged to all the centaurs in common, a gift from Dionysus that had been sealed since the gods placed it there. Opening it was a serious business. Pholus opened it anyway. He thought he could give Hercules a single cup without trouble.

The smell of the wine traveled. It rolled out of the cave mouth and down the mountain like a banner.

By the time they had finished their first cup, the other centaurs were coming. Hercules heard them first — a sound of hooves on stone. Pholus heard them and went pale. He had not thought it through.

The centaurs arrived in a thundering crowd, drunk before they got there from the smell alone. They did not bother to ask whose wine had been opened. They came in armed with branches and rocks. Hercules took up his bow, stepped to the cave mouth, and began to shoot.

He had Hydra-poisoned arrows now. He had been saving them. Tonight he used them.

The centaurs fell where they were hit. One of them was Chiron — the wisest of all centaurs, tutor of heroes, son of Cronos and immortal — and an arrow that had glanced from another centaur struck Chiron in the knee. The wound would not heal. The poison would not let it heal. Chiron, immortal and in unending pain, would later choose to give up his immortality entirely rather than live with that wound; he traded his deathless life to Prometheus and was finally allowed to die.

Pholus, examining a pulled arrow afterward in wonder, turned it in his hand and dropped it on his own foot. He died within the hour. Hercules buried him by the cave mouth and walked on up the mountain in silence.

He found the boar at dawn on a high ridge in deep snow. It was as big as he had been told — the size of a young ox, with tusks the length of his forearm. He drove it from cover by shouting and ran it through the snow until its lungs failed. Boars cannot run in deep powder. The hero could. He chased it in widening circles until the animal's heart was about to give. Then he bound it with rope across its legs, hauled it onto his shoulders, and started down the mountain.

The boar was alive but exhausted. It snorted hot breath against his neck the whole walk. He walked for three days.

When Hercules arrived in Mycenae carrying the boar, Eurystheus saw him from a high window and made it to the bronze jar in time. He climbed in. The lid clanged shut. Hercules stood in the courtyard with the boar across his shoulders, waiting, and when no one came out to receive the animal he set it down in the dust and walked away.

He went back to the room he had been given near the palace and sat for a long time. The labor was complete. The boar was the king's problem now.

In the months that followed he would think of Chiron — the centaur he had grown up hearing stories about, the one who had taught Jason and Achilles to be men — and of the arrow that had been meant for someone else. The Hydra was still killing people through him. He took the poisoned arrows out of his quiver and put them in a smaller pouch, tied tight, kept separate. He used them only when nothing else would do.
