# The Augean Stables

The fifth labor was given as an insult.

King Augeas of Elis owned three thousand cattle. The herd had grown over a generation from a single divine gift; Helios, the sun, had given his son Augeas a herd that did not fall sick and did not die. The cattle thrived. They produced more cattle, which thrived. And every one of them, every day, produced what cattle produce.

The stables had not been cleaned in thirty years. Eurystheus had heard about them and laughed for a long time. He sent for his herald and dictated the labor.

"Go to Elis," the herald read to Hercules. "Clean the stables of King Augeas. In one day."

The court did not laugh this time. The court had stopped laughing at the labors several months ago.

Hercules rode to Elis. He requested an audience with Augeas, who received him in a small throne room that smelled, even there, faintly of the herd. Hercules made his proposal without preamble.

"I will clean the stables in one day," he said. "In exchange you will give me one tenth of the herd."

Augeas laughed. He had not laughed in some time at anything, and he laughed at this for a full minute. He called for his son Phyleus to come witness the bargain. Phyleus came. The deal was struck. Augeas signed. Phyleus signed as witness. Hercules left the palace immediately.

He did not go to the stables first. He went up the valley behind the palace, where two rivers ran — the Alpheus and the Peneus, both fed by snow from the mountains, both fast and cold and full. He spent the morning walking the riverbanks, measuring distances by eye, looking at the slope of the land.

In the afternoon he went down to the stables. He looked at them from the outside. They were as bad as he had been warned. The smell was a presence, almost a creature; it lived in the air a hundred yards out. He saw, on the far side, where the land sloped naturally toward a lower valley.

He picked up a sledge from a barn. He broke a hole through the upstream stable wall. He broke a second hole through the downstream wall. The work took two hours. Then he climbed back up the valley.

The redirection of the Alpheus was the engineering. He dammed the river at its bend with boulders and earth, channeled it toward the new gap in the stable wall, and let it go. The water roared into the stables like a flash flood. He did the same with the Peneus from the other side. Both rivers met inside the stables, swirled together, and went out the downstream gap as a single muddy torrent.

The flood scoured the stables in an afternoon. Every floor, every pillar, every stall. The water went out the downstream side carrying thirty years of waste and a thin slick of straw. By evening the stables were cleaner than they had been on the day they were built.

Hercules took the dams apart. The rivers settled back into their old courses. He walked into the palace at dusk to collect his thousand cattle.

Augeas refused to pay.

"You were sent by Eurystheus," the king said. "You were under orders. You were owed nothing. This was a labor, not a contract."

Phyleus stood up. Phyleus said his father was a liar and the deal had been clear. Augeas, in front of his court, exiled his son on the spot. Phyleus walked out of the palace and did not return.

Hercules walked out too. He did not argue. He had been promised cattle and would not get cattle. He had also cleaned the stables, which counted for something, and he had made a witness who would tell the story.

When he returned to Mycenae and reported the labor done, Eurystheus shouted from the bronze jar. The hero had asked for *pay*. The labor was done for pay, not for the king. The labor did not count.

Hercules waited.

Years later, when the labors were over, he would come back to Elis at the head of an army and kill Augeas in his own throne room. Phyleus would be made king in his father's place. The cattle would be distributed.

But that was a long time off, and for now the king lived, and the labor did not count.
