# The Stymphalian Birds

The sixth labor took Hercules to Lake Stymphalia in Arcadia. The lake was small, fed by springs, and ringed by dense reeds that grew tall enough to hide a man on horseback. For two generations the lake had been the territory of a flock of birds that had crossed from somewhere east — nobody knew where exactly — and made the marshes their own.

They were not ordinary birds. Their beaks were bronze. Their feathers were bronze too, long and sharp as daggers, and they could shake them loose to fall like rain on whatever was below. They ate flesh and they preferred human flesh, when they could get it. The villages around the lake had been emptying for ten years. By the time Hercules arrived, the only people left within a day's walk were a few old shepherds who had refused to leave and kept their flocks in roofed pens.

Eurystheus had picked the labor knowing it was unsolvable. The reeds were too thick to fight in. Arrows could not be drawn back far enough among the stalks. Anyone who waded into the marsh on foot was lost to the mud and the falling feathers within minutes. The birds, when threatened, simply rose and rained metal down on whoever had bothered them. Several heroes had already tried. None had come back.

Hercules walked the shore of the lake for half a day. He looked at the reeds. He climbed the slope on the eastern side and looked down at the water. He thought about the problem until his head ached. Then he sat down at the foot of an oak tree and waited.

He did not know what he was waiting for, exactly. Help, maybe. He had not yet learned to ask for help out loud. But he had learned, in the swamp at Lerna, that some labors did not yield to strength alone, and he was getting better at admitting it.

Athena came down through the branches of the oak in the early evening. She came not as a vision but as a woman walking — gray-eyed, plainly dressed, holding two objects of bronze in her hands. She set them down beside him without speaking. They were krotala — castanets, the kind that priests sometimes used in processions, but enormous. Forged by Hephaestus, the goddess said, when he was younger and still bored.

"Strike them on the ridge," she said. "Not in the marsh. The marsh muffles. The ridge carries. You will not need to wade in."

She was gone before he had finished asking how to thank her.

Hercules took the krotala up to the highest point above the lake at dusk. He waited until the air went still. Then he brought the two pieces of bronze together with all his strength.

The sound came down over the lake like a hammer struck on the bowl of the sky. The reeds shook. The birds rose in a single black cloud of metal, panicked, screaming. Hercules clashed the krotala again. The cloud climbed higher. He could see, in the failing light, individual birds banking and turning, trying to find which direction the sound had come from.

When the flock had risen high enough to clear the reeds, he set down the krotala and picked up his bow. He shot deliberately. Each arrow he loosed found a bird. The Hydra-poisoned shafts, used here for crowd work, killed on contact; the birds fell into the reeds and did not get up.

He shot through his quiver, recovered what arrows he could from the shore, and shot again. By the time it was full dark, he had killed perhaps half the flock. The rest had pulled themselves together into a long line and were heading east, away from the noise. They did not come back to Greece. Sailors reported them later in the Black Sea, on the island of Ares, where Jason and the Argonauts would deal with them when their turn came.

Hercules took the krotala back to the oak at sunrise. He set them at the foot of the tree. They were gone by the time he turned to look again.

He walked into the village near the lake. The old shepherds came out to greet him cautiously, then loudly. He stayed one night, drank their wine, gave the report at Mycenae the next week. Eurystheus accepted the labor.

It was the first labor that had counted on the first attempt.
