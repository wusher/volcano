# The Cretan Bull

The seventh labor sent Hercules across the sea to Crete, and behind the labor lay a story he had to be told in pieces before it made sense.

The bull had been a sign from Poseidon. Years earlier, when Minos was contending for the throne of Crete against his brothers, he had prayed publicly to the sea-god for proof of divine favor. He asked Poseidon to send up a bull from the waves — a bull he would sacrifice immediately to the god, in front of everyone, as both a thanks and a public covenant. Poseidon obliged. A white bull walked out of the surf at Knossos, dripping seawater, perfect. The court was awed. Minos was made king.

Minos kept the bull. He thought it was too beautiful to kill. He sacrificed a lesser bull in its place and put the white one in his own herd.

Poseidon noticed. The god's revenge was complicated and unkind. He drove the bull mad — it broke loose, escaped the palace, and began ravaging the countryside of Crete. He also, by means harder to discuss in polite company, made Minos' wife Pasiphaë fall in love with the same bull. From that union came the Minotaur, who would live the rest of his life in the labyrinth Daedalus had to build for him.

By the time Hercules arrived in Crete, the bull had been loose on the island for years. Smoke came from its nostrils. Salt foamed at its mouth. It killed shepherds. It uprooted olive trees. King Minos was an aging man by now, embarrassed by the whole business, and openly grateful when Hercules' ship pulled into the harbor.

"Take it," Minos said. "Take it however you like. I'll give you anything you need. Men, horses, weapons."

"I'll take it alone," Hercules said. Eurystheus had been explicit on that point, and the hero had learned that following the letter of the king's orders mattered more than performing them well.

Minos shrugged and showed him to a guest house. Hercules slept twelve hours, ate, and went out the next morning into the countryside to find the animal.

He spent a week tracking. The bull's path through the olive groves was easy to follow — broken branches, churned earth, a few unfortunate sheep. Hercules followed without hurry. He found the bull at midday, drinking at a stream, alone.

What followed was not a battle exactly. It was a wrestling match between a man wearing the pelt of an uncuttable lion and a bull that had been thrown out of the sea by a god. The bull came at him; Hercules took it by the horns. The animal threw him in a complete arc. He landed on his back, breath gone, and got up. The bull came again. He caught it again. Three times the bull threw him. The fourth time, he held.

He pinned its horns into the dust with both hands and pushed. Their faces were close enough that he could smell the seawater still coming from the bull's nostrils after all these years. He held the position until the animal's strength broke. Then he tied it.

He led it to the shore at the end of a long rope. The bull came docilely now, head down. Hercules swam it back to the Greek mainland behind the boat, the rope tied to a stern cleat, the animal paddling along like a tired dog.

The bull made it across alive. He led it to Mycenae and brought it to the courtyard. Eurystheus, from inside the bronze jar, accepted the labor. Then he refused to keep the animal.

"Turn it loose," the king called from the jar. "I will not be responsible for it. It's not my bull."

Hercules untied the rope. The bull stood in the courtyard for some time, looking at nothing in particular. Then it walked out of the gate, turned north, and started up the coast. It would run wild through Greece for years to come — through Attica, into Marathon — until the hero Theseus, years later, on his way to becoming king of Athens, would meet it on the plain of Marathon and kill it cleanly with a single blow.

The labor counted. Hercules ate a real meal for the first time in weeks and slept until the next labor was named.
