# The Mares of Diomedes

The eighth labor sent Hercules north, across the Aegean and up into Thrace, to fetch the four mares belonging to King Diomedes.

The mares were horses in the technical sense only. Diomedes fed them on the flesh of his guests. He kept them in iron tethers in a marble stable, because no rope could hold them, and he had developed a custom — over years, with growing enthusiasm — of inviting travelers into his palace, feeding them well, and then in the night having them dragged to the stables for the horses to consume. The mares lived on human flesh. They would not eat anything else.

Hercules took a small band of companions for this one. The crew included a young man named Abderus, a son of Hermes, who had become a favorite of the hero's during the previous year's travels. Abderus was good-hearted, easily impressed, and very young. He would not have been Hercules' first pick for a fight, but the boy had asked to come and Hercules had not had the heart to refuse.

They sailed up the coast and came ashore at night, two miles from the palace. They moved quietly. The stables were on the seaward side of the palace, which was the kind of arrangement that Diomedes had chosen for convenience and that now turned out to be his mistake.

Hercules and three of his men got into the stables and killed the grooms before they could shout. The mares were exactly as bad as the stories had said. They had clear, intelligent eyes and they screamed in the way that a horse screams only when it is excited rather than afraid. Hercules cut their iron tethers with the same hand-axe he had been carrying since Lerna and drove them out the stable gate toward the beach.

The mares ran. They were used to running in a circle inside the stables; outside, in open air, with no walls to turn them, they ran in a stampede. Hercules and his men ran behind them with torches, herding them down the slope to the shore.

That was when Diomedes' household woke up.

The king's warriors came pouring out of the palace, half-armored, in the panic of men who have not been attacked in their own house in a generation. Hercules turned to meet them. He told Abderus to hold the mares on the beach. "Just keep them there," he said. "Don't let them go back inland. We'll deal with the king and come back."

Abderus said yes. Hercules turned and ran up the slope with the rest of the men to meet the warriors.

The fight at the palace gate took longer than it should have. Diomedes himself came out, fought for some time, and was eventually wrestled to the ground and bound. His warriors broke and fled. Hercules dragged the king down to the beach, planning to make a public end of him and load the mares onto the ship.

He came over the rise above the beach and stopped.

The mares had eaten Abderus. There was very little left.

Hercules stood for some time. Then he carried Diomedes the rest of the way down to the shore, where the mares were standing in a loose group, blood on their muzzles and a kind of calm in their eyes. He cut the king's bonds. He set Diomedes on his feet. Then he turned and walked away ten paces.

The king ran. The mares ran after the king. They caught him before he was even halfway to the dunes. They ate him on the sand in front of his own palace.

When they were done, the mares were a different kind of animal. The taste of their master had broken something in them. They walked back to Hercules calm as colts. They followed him to the ship. They let themselves be loaded onto the deck without complaint.

Hercules buried what he could find of Abderus at the high-water line. Years later, when the labors were over, he would come back and build a city on that spot. He would name it Abdera. The city would be famous for a long time.

He took the mares to Mycenae. Eurystheus accepted the labor, then turned the animals loose on Mount Olympus, where the wild beasts eventually finished them.
