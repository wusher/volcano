# The Belt of Hippolyta

The ninth labor came at the request of Eurystheus' daughter.

Admete was sixteen and ambitious. She had heard travelers' descriptions of the war-belt of Hippolyta, queen of the Amazons — a band of golden mail, worked all over with figures of horses and lions, given to Hippolyta by her father Ares as proof of her right to lead the women's army. Admete wanted it. Her father, eager both to please his daughter and to send Hercules into a fight he might genuinely lose, made the labor.

"The belt of Hippolyta," the herald said. "Brought to my daughter by your hand. Or it doesn't count."

Hercules took a small fleet for this one. The crew included some of the best men of the age — Theseus, who would later become king of Athens, and Telamon, and a few others who knew their way around a boat. They sailed up the coast of Asia Minor and around the Black Sea until they came at last to Themiscyra, the capital of the Amazons. The city sat on the bank of the Thermodon river, broad and quiet, with horses grazing on the plain beyond.

Hippolyta met them on the beach.

She had heard he was coming — word travels even to the Amazons — and she came down to the shore herself with a small escort of warriors. She was Hercules' height almost exactly. She had black hair cut short and a face that was used to making decisions. The belt was at her waist. The figures on it caught the morning sun.

They talked on the sand for some time. Hercules explained the labor. He did not try to wrap it in flattery; he told her plainly that her belt had been asked for, by name, by a girl in Mycenae who had heard of it from her father. Hippolyta listened. She asked about the labors. She asked, with what Hercules later realized was real interest, about him.

By the end of the conversation, she had agreed to give him the belt. Freely. As a gift, in honor of his work, and as a courtesy between equals. She invited the crew up to the city for a meal.

Hera, watching this from above, would not have it.

The goddess had been working against Hercules from before his birth and had not stopped working against him for a day in forty years. She saw the labor about to end without a single blow struck and the hero coming home with a friend, possibly more than a friend, definitely with the belt. She came down disguised as an Amazon. She moved through the camp.

She started a rumor and ran with it. *The Greeks are taking the queen. They have her. They are forcing her onto the ship.* She said it loudly. She said it to women who were already nervous about the Greek ships on the beach. The story spread in minutes.

The Amazons attacked.

They came down the slope toward the beach in formation. Hercules, on the sand with Hippolyta, looked up and saw a charge coming and could not understand what had happened. He had been talking with the queen ten seconds ago. He looked at Hippolyta. He saw — for an instant — the queen's face shift toward shock, toward the same confusion. They both understood at the same moment that something had been done to them, by someone, that they could not undo.

He drew his sword anyway. He had to. The Amazons were ten yards away.

What happened next was the kind of decision that gets called a mistake in the songs and a survival in the histories. Hercules, certain of betrayal — because the alternative was unthinkable — struck Hippolyta down where she stood. He took the belt from her body. He cut his way back to the ship with his men around him, fighting through women who would never in their lives know that they had been moved like puppets.

The ship pulled away from the beach as the tide turned. Hercules sat on the deck with the belt across his knees and did not speak for two days.

It was on the voyage home that one of the men finally told him — having pieced it together from things he had seen and not understood at the time — that there had been an Amazon in the camp who shouldn't have been there. A face nobody recognized. A voice that started everything. Hercules knew the face from the description without needing to be told the name.

He delivered the belt to Admete in person at Mycenae. The girl received it politely. He did not speak of the labor for a long time after that. Decades later, when the labors were over and he had begun to tell some of the stories to younger heroes, this was the one he still would not tell.
