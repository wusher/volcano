# The Cattle of Geryon

The tenth labor took Hercules to the end of the world.

Geryon was a three-bodied giant. Three torsos joined at the waist, three heads above them, six arms, six legs from the hip down. He lived on Erytheia, an island off the far western coast of what the Greeks then called the inhabited world — somewhere past the Pillars that Hercules had not yet set up. He owned a herd of red cattle, the most beautiful cattle anyone had ever heard of, and the herd was guarded by Orthrus, a two-headed dog who was a brother of Cerberus, and by a herdsman named Eurytion, a giant in his own right.

Eurystheus chose the labor with care. Bringing the cattle back would require crossing more sea and more land than any Greek had crossed before. The hero might not return at all.

Hercules left in spring. The first leg of the journey took him across Libya. The sun there is not the sun Greeks were used to; it presses down on the back of the neck like a hand. He walked for weeks. His water was always running low. At one point, on a stretch of desert without shade, Hercules drew his bow and pointed it up at the sun itself.

"Stand off," he shouted. "Or I will put a shaft through you."

Helios, in his chariot above, looked down at this. The god was old enough to have seen a great many ridiculous things, and he chose to be amused. He lowered the chariot. He spoke with Hercules briefly. Then he lent the hero his own golden cup — the great bowl that the sun used to sail back east each night, beneath the ocean, after his day's run.

In that cup Hercules sailed across the outer sea to Erytheia.

The island was green. Mist hung in the valleys. The cattle were in a wide pasture below a low hill. Orthrus saw him first and came running, both heads barking, jaws splitting wide. Hercules killed the dog with a single blow from his club. He killed Eurytion the same way a few moments later when the herdsman came running.

Then Geryon came.

The giant was every bit as strange as the stories had said. The three bodies moved together, took breaths together, raised three shields and three spears together. Hercules took aim with one of his Hydra-tipped arrows and shot once, very carefully. The arrow passed through the side of the first body, through the second, and into the third, all in a single line. Geryon fell forward as one piece and did not get up. The hero had killed three men with one shaft.

He took the cattle.

The journey back was longer than the journey out. He drove the herd east along the coast of Spain. He set up two stone pillars on either side of the strait between Africa and Europe to mark where he had passed — what later sailors would call the Pillars of Hercules, and what later mapmakers would name as Gibraltar and Ceuta. Then he drove the herd through Gaul, through the Alps, down into Italy.

Hera, as always, would not let it end quietly. She sent a swarm of gadflies in the plains of Thrace that scattered the cattle across the countryside. Hercules spent weeks rounding them up. He found nearly all of them. A few wandered off into the hills of southeastern Europe and started feral herds there that lasted for centuries.

He came back to Mycenae with the herd intact. The labor was unmistakably complete. Eurystheus, by now resigned, accepted it from the bronze jar. Then he sacrificed every one of the red cattle to Hera, in a public ceremony that took most of a day. The goddess had been beaten in this round and the king felt that a thorough offering was the right thing to do.

Hercules attended the sacrifice. He stood in the back of the courtyard and watched the smoke rise. The cattle he had walked halfway across the known world to bring back were eaten in a single afternoon by the priests and the army. He did not say anything. He went home to the room that had been his for ten years now and slept until the next labor was named.

Two more to go.
