# The Apples of the Hesperides

For the eleventh labor Eurystheus moved the goalposts.

By the count anyone would do honestly, Hercules should have been finished. He had killed the lion, killed the Hydra, captured the hind, captured the boar, cleaned the stables, scattered the birds, taken the bull, brought the mares, brought the belt, and driven the herd of Geryon halfway across the world. That was ten. But the king had rejected two labors on procedural grounds — the Hydra for outside help, the stables for taking pay — and so two more had been added to the list. Hercules accepted this without argument. Arguing was not a thing he did anymore.

The eleventh labor was the golden apples of the Hesperides.

The Hesperides were nymphs — three of them, daughters of evening, who tended a tree in a walled garden at the western edge of the world. The tree had been a wedding gift from Gaia to Hera. It bore apples of gold. The garden was guarded by a hundred-headed dragon named Ladon, who never slept, because every time one head closed its eyes another opened.

No one knew where the garden actually was. The Hesperides were called the Daughters of Evening because their garden was somewhere in the direction of the sunset, which is information without being information. Hercules had to find them before he could begin.

He wandered for months. He went west, by sea and by land, asking everyone he could find. The answers were always vague and disagreed with one another. He eventually came on Nereus, an old sea-god famous for knowing the geography of distant places and famously unwilling to share what he knew. Nereus could change shape. Hercules wrestled him — through fire, water, animal forms — and held on until the god, exhausted and undignified, finally told him the way.

On the road west, after he had directions, he came on Prometheus.

The Titan was chained to a rock in the Caucasus, where Zeus had left him a long time ago for the crime of giving fire to mortals. An eagle came every day to eat his liver. The liver regrew overnight. The cycle had been going on for centuries.

Hercules looked at Prometheus for some time. Then he raised his bow, took one of the Hydra-poisoned arrows from the small pouch where he kept them, and shot the eagle out of the sky. He broke Prometheus' chains with his club.

Prometheus, free for the first time in a long time, sat for a while breathing. Then he gave Hercules a piece of advice.

"Don't get the apples yourself," he said. "You can't. Only Atlas can pick them. He's their grandfather, basically. Send him."

"And how do I get Atlas to do it?"

"Hold up the sky for him while he goes."

Hercules walked on west. He found Atlas at the far edge of the world, where the Titan stood as he had stood for a thousand years with the dome of the sky on his shoulders. Atlas looked tired. He was, in fact, very tired.

Hercules made his proposal. Atlas considered. Atlas accepted. The transfer of the sky from one set of shoulders to another took both of them grunting in concert. Hercules took the weight. Atlas, freed for the first time in his memory, walked off toward the garden. Hercules watched him go.

Atlas was gone for some time. He came back with three apples in one hand.

He stood looking at Hercules holding up the sky. He decided that he did not want to hold up the sky anymore. He said so.

"I'll take the apples to Mycenae myself," he said. "You stay here."

Hercules thought quickly. He nodded, agreeable.

"Of course," he said. "Just hold the sky for me for one moment, would you? I need to put a pad on my shoulders. The weight is hard on the bone."

Atlas considered. Atlas was strong but he was not, as Prometheus had implied, clever. He set the apples down and shouldered the sky back. Hercules stepped out from under the weight, picked up the apples, said thank you, and walked east.

Atlas was furious, but his hands were full. He could not chase.

Hercules brought the apples to Mycenae. Eurystheus refused to keep them — gold apples of a goddess were not the kind of thing a mortal king was supposed to own. Hercules took them back to the garden, where Athena, in time, returned them to the tree.

One more labor.
