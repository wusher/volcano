# Cerberus

The twelfth and final labor was the one Eurystheus had been saving.

He gave it to Hercules in the great hall, with the court watching, and for once he did not hide in the bronze jar. He sat on his throne and delivered the labor straight.

"Bring me Cerberus," he said. "Alive. Out of the underworld. Without weapons."

The court was silent. Cerberus was the three-headed dog who guarded the gates of the dead. He was Orthrus' brother. He was Typhon and Echidna's son, like the lion and the Hydra had been, but he was older and bigger than either of them. He had a serpent for a tail and a mane of writhing snakes around each of his three necks. The dead came in past him daily. None of them ever went back out.

Hercules nodded. He turned and walked out of the hall.

He spent two weeks preparing. He visited a priest at Eleusis and was initiated into the Mysteries — the rites that gave a living person some claim to passage in the world below. He fasted. He told his old friend Theseus, by letter, what he was about to attempt.

The entrance to the underworld he chose was at Taenarum, a sea-cave at the southern tip of the Peloponnese where a vent of damp air came up from somewhere deep. Hercules walked in alone, in the morning. He left his weapons on a ledge near the entrance, except for his lion-skin and his hands. He walked downward.

The path went down for a long time. The light changed. He came at last to the bank of the Styx, where Charon the ferryman waited with his pole. Charon, who was not used to having a living passenger demand transit, refused.

Hercules stared at him for a long moment. Charon, who had been a ferryman since before time, looked into the hero's face and decided the easier option was to let him on. (Hades would later punish Charon for this lapse, chaining him for a year. But that was Hades' problem and Charon's. For now: the boat moved.)

On the far shore Hercules walked through the gloom and met old friends.

He met the shade of Meleager, who had once hunted the Calydonian Boar with him, and the two of them spoke for a long time. Meleager asked, in passing, if Hercules might marry his sister Deianira when he got back to the upper world. Hercules said yes. He did not yet know what that promise would mean.

He met Theseus and Pirithous, who were trapped in stone chairs at the edge of the king's domain — they had come down to try to kidnap Persephone and had been caught for their hubris. Hercules pulled Theseus free with the strength of one arm; the earth held Pirithous and would not let him go. Theseus and Hercules embraced. Pirithous stayed.

At last Hercules came to the throne of Hades. He bowed. He explained the labor with the same flat directness he had used a hundred times before in Mycenae.

Hades, who liked clear deals, considered.

"You may take him," the god said. "On one condition. No weapons. Subdue him with your hands. And bring him back unhurt."

Hercules agreed. He walked to the gates.

Cerberus was lying across the threshold like a wall. He saw Hercules and the three heads came up in a single motion. He bellowed. Hercules walked straight into him.

The fight was wordless. Hercules got his arms around the three necks at once, pinned the snakes of the mane down with his elbows, and squeezed. He held the hold while the dog thrashed. The serpent of the tail wrapped his leg and bit through the lion-skin into his thigh — the only wound he took that day. He held the hold. After a long time Cerberus stopped fighting and lay down with his three heads on Hercules' shoulder, gasping.

Hercules carried him up.

He came out of the cave at Taenarum at dusk, the great dog trotting beside him on a leash made of his own belt. He walked north to Mycenae. People in the villages he passed through saw the dog and ran.

When he brought Cerberus into the courtyard at Mycenae, Eurystheus saw the animal and made it to the bronze jar in time. From inside, in a voice that had given up entirely on dignity, the king accepted the labor and asked Hercules to please take the dog away.

Hercules took Cerberus back to the cave at Taenarum and set him at the gates again. The dog, calm now, lay back down across the threshold.

The hero walked out of the cave into ordinary daylight. Behind him: twelve labors. Ahead of him: the rest of his life, whatever that turned out to be.

He went home.
